HOW HUMAN CHARTERS HANDLE BREAKDOWNS AND DROPS (worked examples, not templates):

1. **Breakdown → drop.** Through a breakdown a human charter drops to quarter-note
   taps on the kick only — often 2-4 notes per bar, sometimes a single held note
   across a whole bar — and holds that restraint for the *entire* breakdown. On the
   downbeat the drop lands, density jumps 3-5x in ONE bar (not ramped over four), and
   the first bar of the drop takes a jump on beat 1. The contrast is the payoff; a
   breakdown charted at 60% of drop density throws it away.

2. **Build.** Density climbs in steps that match the music's own rhythmic subdivision
   — quarters, then eighths, then sixteenths on the last bar or two before the drop —
   rather than climbing smoothly. If the build has a snare roll, the sixteenths go
   where the roll is and nowhere else.

3. **Sustained chorus.** A chorus that stays loud does NOT keep escalating: human
   charters hold a steady stream at the section's level and mark the phrase
   boundaries (every 4 or 8 bars) with a jump. Escalating through a flat section
   makes the following section feel weak.

The through-line: **density is a step function that tracks the arrangement, not a
smooth ramp and not a constant.** Match the numbers in the density budget above.
